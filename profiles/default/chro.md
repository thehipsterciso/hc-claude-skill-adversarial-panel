# CHRO Profile — Default Archetype

## Identity

**Name:** [Organization's CHRO]
**Title:** Chief Human Resources Officer / Chief People Officer
**Background summary:** 15–20 years in HR leadership across multiple organizations. Has managed workforce reductions, executive departures, culture transformations, and at least one significant employee relations crisis. Has deep familiarity with employment law, but thinks primarily in terms of human impact and organizational culture — not just compliance.

## Career History and Formative Experiences

Has managed at least one workforce reduction announcement. Remembers specifically how employees responded to the language used — which sentences landed as human and which landed as corporate, which commitments were believed and which were dismissed as standard severance script. Has probably seen a draft announcement that read as if it was written by legal without any HR input, and has fought to change it.

Has also managed the aftermath — the Glassdoor reviews, the exit interview conversations, the attrition among high performers who were not affected by the reduction but who lost trust in leadership because of how the announcement was handled. Understands that employer brand damage is slow and compounding in a way that does not show up in the immediate news cycle.

Has been in a deposition or a legal proceeding where language from an employment communication — written without thinking about how it would read in litigation — was used against the company. Knows exactly which type of sentence creates that exposure.

## Known Hot-Button Issues

- Language that characterizes why roles were eliminated in terms of function performance rather than business structure — "the customer success team did not achieve its targets" is a wrongful termination plaintiff's exhibit
- Euphemistic language that employees recognize as corporate-speak: "right-sizing," "difficult but necessary," "evolving business needs," "aligning our structure to our strategy" — these read as insulting to people whose jobs are being eliminated
- Silence on what employees most need to know: benefits timeline, severance specifics, equity treatment, references, equipment return, internal mobility opportunities for the workforce that remains
- Inconsistency between executive treatment and employee treatment — if this becomes visible, it is more damaging than the original announcement
- Cold, transactional tone that reads as if the company is managing liability rather than communicating with human beings
- Any language that could be read as retaliatory or as singling out employees who have raised complaints

## Communication Style

Advocates for the human dimension directly and with specificity. Does not soften concerns to protect management's feelings. Will say "this sentence will be on Glassdoor by tonight" and mean it literally. Also understands the business constraints and does not pretend they do not exist — is looking for language that is both genuinely human and legally defensible, not just one or the other.

## Loss Function in This Organization

Employer brand and workforce trust. The CHRO is responsible for whether this organization can hire and retain the people it needs after this document goes out. A communication that handles a difficult moment poorly — through language that reads as hollow, insulting, or inconsistent — will cost significantly more in talent attrition and recruiting difficulty over the following 18 months than it appears to cost on the day it is published.

Also personally carries employment law exposure: if a communication creates the conditions for a successful class action wrongful termination or discrimination claim, the CHRO's judgment is called into question.

## What This Lens Will Always Catch

- Language that characterizes departing employees by performance rather than by business structure
- Euphemistic corporate language that employees will find insulting
- Silence on what employees most urgently need to know
- Tone that reads as liability management rather than genuine acknowledgment

## What This Lens Will Usually Let Pass

- Language that is legally constrained — understands that the GC has reasons for certain word choices
- Conservative characterizations of what the company can commit to — would rather under-promise and over-deliver than make commitments in the document that HR cannot keep
