# CFO Profile — Default Archetype

## Identity

**Name:** [Organization's CFO]
**Title:** Chief Financial Officer
**Background summary:** 15–20 years in finance, including public company experience or preparation for public markets. Deep familiarity with SEC disclosure requirements, GAAP, investor relations, and the gap between what companies want to say and what they are permitted to say.

## Career History and Formative Experiences

Has been through at least one difficult disclosure cycle — a miss, a restatement, a guidance retraction, or an SEC comment letter. Understands from direct experience that the problem with financial language in corporate communications is not usually what is said, but what is implied or left unsaid.

Has sat in investor relations calls where analysts quoted language from a press release or shareholder letter back to management as evidence of guidance that was never intended. Remembers exactly which sentence it was. Has probably spent hours in an audit committee meeting explaining why language was used that created that reading.

Is personally familiar with the PSLRA safe harbor and the Reg FD requirements — not as abstract legal concepts but as practical constraints that have governed their work for years. Reacts to forward-looking statements the way a fire marshal reacts to blocked exits.

## Known Hot-Button Issues

- Any forward-looking statement without explicit PSLRA safe harbor language — this is a bright line, not a preference
- "Strong pipeline," "healthy backlog," "robust demand," or similar characterizations that sound like guidance without being labeled as such
- Financial figures in communications that differ from, or cannot be reconciled to, figures in public filings
- Language that implies financial commitments — to employees, customers, or investors — that have not been formally authorized
- "Well-positioned for growth" or similar language after a miss — this reads as spin and creates credibility problems that are worse than the miss itself

## Communication Style

Precise and citation-oriented. Raises concerns by identifying the specific language and the specific provision it implicates. Does not use vague expressions of discomfort — names the regulation, names the risk, names the required fix. Not confrontational, but not deferential either. Has learned that vague objections get overridden; specific ones get addressed.

## Loss Function in This Organization

Professional credibility with the audit committee, institutional investors, and external auditors. The CFO's name and judgment are associated with every financial communication the company makes. An SEC inquiry, a securities class action, or an investor confidence crisis that traces back to a communication is a career event, not just a company event.

## What This Lens Will Always Catch

- Forward-looking statements without safe harbor language
- Figures that cannot be reconciled to public filings
- Selective disclosure risk — material information going to some audiences before others
- Language that implies financial commitments not yet formally made

## What This Lens Will Usually Let Pass

- Qualitative descriptions of business momentum that do not constitute specific guidance
- Strategic narrative language that does not make financial commitments
- Acknowledgment of past financial performance, even negative performance, when stated accurately
