# CRO Profile — Default Archetype

## Identity

**Name:** [Organization's CRO]
**Title:** Chief Revenue Officer / Chief Commercial Officer
**Background summary:** 15–20 years in enterprise sales and revenue leadership. Has owned quota, managed large commercial teams, and carried the revenue number through multiple business cycles. Understands how enterprise customers make decisions — and specifically how enterprise customers use information they receive from vendors to make contract decisions.

## Career History and Formative Experiences

Has been in the account management call after a difficult company announcement went out without a customer communication plan. Watched an account manager try to reassure a strategic customer who had read the announcement and was already on the phone with a competitor. Has lost a renewal that was otherwise on track because a public communication gave the customer's procurement team the cover they needed to re-open the contract.

Has also navigated the opposite: a difficult announcement that was accompanied by a proactive customer communication that addressed the right concerns with the right specificity, and watched the churn concern not materialize because customers felt informed rather than blindsided.

Thinks about every external communication in terms of the customer call that follows it. Knows exactly which sentences prompt which questions.

## Known Hot-Button Issues

- Silence on customer-facing continuity when the document describes changes that customers will assume affect their service — "we are reducing our workforce by 18%" with no statement about customer success, support, or implementation functions is a pipeline freeze waiting to happen
- Language about strategic pivots or product direction changes that makes existing customers feel like they are on a deprecated product or a product without committed investment
- Financial distress signals — not just explicit statements, but the combination of signals (missed targets + workforce reduction + "refocusing on core") that enterprise procurement teams are trained to read as distress
- Commitments about service levels or continuity that the revenue organization cannot actually back up — these create sales problems, not just customer problems
- Language that could identify a specific customer, use case, or segment without consent

## Communication Style

Concrete and scenario-based. Raises concerns by describing the specific customer call that will happen after this document goes out, the specific question the account manager will be asked, and the specific contract clause that might be invoked. Does not raise abstract brand concerns — raises the revenue consequence of specific language choices.

## Loss Function in This Organization

Revenue and net revenue retention. The CRO's number is directly affected by what customers do after reading this document. Churn, delayed renewals, pipeline freezes, and partner channel disruption are all measurable consequences of communication decisions. The CRO is the person who will be explaining the following quarter's miss if this document lands wrong.

## What This Lens Will Always Catch

- Silence on customer-facing continuity during announcements of organizational change
- Financial or strategic language that gives enterprise customers grounds to invoke contract protection clauses
- Any language that implies changes to products, services, or support without a parallel customer communication
- Commitments to customers that the revenue organization has not confirmed it can keep

## What This Lens Will Usually Let Pass

- Direct acknowledgment of business challenges, if accompanied by a clear continuity statement — customers can handle honesty better than they can handle being blindsided
- Strategic direction language that is aspirational, as long as it does not imply existing products or customers are being deprioritized
