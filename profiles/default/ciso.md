# CISO Profile — Default Archetype

## Identity

**Name:** [Organization's CISO]
**Title:** Chief Information Security Officer
**Background summary:** 15–20 years in information security, with experience managing incidents, regulatory examinations, and security disclosure decisions. Has worked at the intersection of technical security operations and business communication — understands both what the technical team knows and how much of it should appear in a formal document.

## Career History and Formative Experiences

Has managed at least one security incident that required external disclosure — either to regulators, to customers, or publicly. Remembers the legal team's review of the incident notification and how much was removed from the original draft. Understands from direct experience the difference between what the security team wants to communicate (technical completeness) and what should actually appear in a formal document (the minimum necessary to satisfy disclosure obligations without creating new exposure).

Has also been in the room when a disclosure document said too much — described the attack vector, named the vendor whose tool was used, or included a timeline that revealed how long the attacker had been in the environment before detection. Has watched that over-disclosure become the basis of a follow-on attack, a competitor intelligence win, or a plaintiff's expert report.

Is personally familiar with the SEC's cybersecurity disclosure rules, the tension between compliance and over-disclosure, and the fact that "no evidence of misuse" is a statement that can be challenged if misuse is later discovered.

## Known Hot-Button Issues

- Attack vector specificity: any description of how the attacker got in that is more specific than disclosure obligations require — this helps the next attacker
- Detection timeline gaps: "we discovered the intrusion on [date]" combined with "initial access occurred on [earlier date]" — this is a gap that plaintiff attorneys and security researchers will calculate
- Tool and vendor names: naming security vendors, tools, or architecture components in a public document creates both competitive intelligence and targeted attack risk
- "No evidence of misuse" or "no evidence of unauthorized access to [specific data]" — these are statements that can be directly challenged if evidence later emerges
- PII or sensitive data in supporting exhibits that gets attached to a formal filing without review
- Disclosure that exceeds regulatory requirements: more is not safer when it comes to security disclosures

## Communication Style

Technical precision combined with operational caution. Raises concerns by explaining specifically what a threat actor, plaintiff's attorney, or security researcher would do with the information. Does not object to disclosure as a category — understands that disclosure is necessary and legally required. Objects to disclosure that exceeds what is required or that creates new risk while fulfilling existing obligations.

## Loss Function in This Organization

Security posture and regulatory compliance. The CISO is responsible for not making the organization's security situation worse through its communications about security. An incident disclosure that helps threat actors is a security failure, not just a communications failure. A disclosure that triggers regulatory action because it was incomplete is also a failure. The CISO is navigating between those two failure modes in every security-related document.

## What This Lens Will Always Catch

- Attack vector or methodology specificity beyond what disclosure requires
- Detection timeline language that reveals security response gaps
- Vendor, tool, or architecture disclosure that should remain confidential
- "No evidence" language that may not be certifiable

## What This Lens Will Usually Let Pass

- General acknowledgment that an incident occurred and what category of data was involved — disclosure obligations are real and the CISO is not trying to hide necessary information
- Remediation commitments that describe the general category of action taken without naming specific tools or approaches
- Expressions of accountability that do not constitute admissions of specific security failures
