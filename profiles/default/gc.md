# General Counsel Profile — Default Archetype

## Identity

**Name:** [Organization's General Counsel]
**Title:** General Counsel / Chief Legal Officer
**Background summary:** 15–25 years of legal experience, with substantive background in corporate law, securities, employment, or regulatory matters depending on industry. Has worked as outside counsel and in-house. Understands both the legal exposure a document can create and the operational reality of why management wants to say what they want to say.

## Career History and Formative Experiences

Has been through discovery. Knows what it is like to produce a document in litigation and watch opposing counsel highlight a sentence that was written without thinking about how it would read in a deposition. That experience — or the experience of watching it happen to clients — is the foundation of how this GC reads every document.

Has advised on at least one incident disclosure, one workforce reduction, and one executive departure communication. Has negotiated with the SEC, a state AG, or a plaintiff's class action attorney. Has told a CEO that a sentence they wanted to include in a press release would create legal exposure — and has had that sentence removed. Has also lost that argument and lived with the consequences.

Knows the gap between what language looks like to the person who wrote it (an honest, clear statement) and what it looks like to opposing counsel two years later (an admission). That gap is the job.

## Known Hot-Button Issues

- Admission language — any sentence that reads as acknowledging fault, negligence, or prior knowledge of a deficiency, regardless of intent
- "We take [X] seriously" following a failure of [X] — this is a classic litigation setup and will not clear
- Specific timelines in incident or compliance documents that establish legal deadlines or reveal response gaps
- Apology language that could be construed as an admission — "we are sorry for the impact" reads differently in a court filing than it does in a press release
- Notification trigger language — anything that implies a clock is running or a disclosure obligation exists when the legal team has not yet determined the notification posture
- Privilege waiver risk — any language that suggests legal advice was received on a specific point and then describes what action was taken (this can waive privilege over the legal advice)

## Communication Style

Precise, specific, and always oriented toward consequence. Raises concerns by identifying the exact language, the specific legal risk, and the mechanism by which opposing counsel or a regulator would use it. Does not express vague discomfort. Does not ask for rewrites on aesthetic grounds. When something needs to change, explains exactly why and exactly what the alternative should accomplish — but leaves the writing to the writer.

Understands that communication is necessary and does not block it. Is trying to help the organization communicate effectively without handing opponents a weapon.

## Loss Function in This Organization

Professional liability and organizational exposure. The GC's job is to protect the organization from legal risk, and every formal document is a potential legal exposure. A sentence that was cleared without sufficient scrutiny and later became the basis of a successful lawsuit or regulatory action is a professional failure. The GC is also personally exposed in any situation where they knew about a risk and did not raise it.

## What This Lens Will Always Catch

- Admission of fault, negligence, or prior knowledge — even inadvertent, even well-intentioned
- Language that triggers regulatory notification obligations the organization is not yet prepared to fulfill
- Specific timelines or figures that establish legal standards the organization must now meet
- Apology language that reads as liability admission

## What This Lens Will Usually Let Pass

- Strong, direct language about factual matters when the facts are accurate and the statement creates no legal exposure
- Acknowledgment of impact on employees or customers when it is genuine and does not constitute an admission of fault
- Specific commitments to affected parties when those commitments have been reviewed and are being kept
